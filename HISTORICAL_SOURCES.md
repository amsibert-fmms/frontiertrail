# Historical Source Notes for Frontier Trail

This document captures curated historical details from the user-provided source material to support future simulation balancing, event-writing, and flavor text.

## Core Journey Realities (1840s–1880s)

- Most overland journeys covered roughly **2,000 miles** and often took around **five months** by wagon.
- Typical daily progress was often around **15–20 miles**, depending on weather, terrain, livestock condition, and road quality.
- Emigrants generally launched from **Independence, Missouri** and timed departure for spring to avoid both poor grazing and mountain snow.
- A large wagon typically required at least **three yoke (pairs) of oxen**; oxen were slower than mules but usually more reliable.

## Daily Life and Workload

- Camp life often followed a strict schedule: pre-dawn wake-up, livestock roundup, breakfast, wagon loading, travel, nooning halt, evening camp setup, then guard shifts.
- Men more often handled draft animals, repairs, and hunting; women often carried a heavier share of cooking, cleaning, childcare, and camp logistics.
- Typical maintenance tasks included wheel lubrication, axle repair, re-shoeing animals, and recovery from mud, storms, or steep grades.

## Risks and Mortality Drivers

- Major hazards were usually **accidents and disease** rather than combat.
- River crossings were among the most dangerous events due to drowning risk and wagon/supply loss.
- Cholera and other diseases spread quickly in camped groups with limited medical access.
- Firearm accidents, stampedes, wagon run-overs, and weather exposure were recurring causes of injury and death.

## Native Nations and Trail Interaction

- Many emigrants feared Native attack, but multiple historical summaries indicate disease/accident mortality far exceeded conflict mortality.
- Trail interactions frequently included trade, guidance, ferriage/river help, and local knowledge exchange.
- Source excerpts for the Bear River corridor repeatedly describe contact with Shoshone/Bannock people in a mix of trade, observation, and occasional tension.

## Bear River / Thomas Fork Corridor Notes

The user-provided excerpts repeatedly describe the Bear River Valley as:

- an important rest-and-recovery stretch after harder, drier segments,
- rich in grass and water,
- scenic (streams, mountain fir, occasional snow patches),
- mosquito-heavy in warm periods,
- and operationally significant for bridge tolls, fords, and stock recovery.

Recurring details from diary excerpts include:

- steep descent/ascent logistics (locked wheels, rope braking),
- weather shifts (rain and fresh mountain snow),
- stampedes and night guard duty,
- plentiful trout/fish in some reaches,
- and pause-days to rest cattle and teams.

4. **Mosquito/heat discomfort effects** on morale, sleep quality, and illness probability.
5. **Trail role asymmetry events** that reflect different recorded experiences in men's vs women's diary accounts.
6. **Cultural encounter events** emphasizing trade/guidance outcomes, not only conflict framing.
